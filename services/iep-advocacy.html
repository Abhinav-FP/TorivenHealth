IEP & School Advocacy Support — Ontario

Helping Families Navigate School Systems With Confidence

Navigating school supports can feel overwhelming for many families. Understanding Individual Education Plans (IEPs), preparing for school meetings, and ensuring that accommodations are properly implemented can be complex and stressful. Our School Advocacy & IEP Support Service helps families better understand the educational system and advocate for appropriate supports for their child.

This is a consultation and guidance service — not legal representation. Clinicians provide information, preparation support, and communication guidance to help families navigate the school system more confidently.

Book a Guidance Call See Session Structure

What Families Often Experience

The school system can feel difficult to navigate alone.

IEP documents can be long, technical, and hard to interpret. School meetings can feel one-sided — with educators and administrators who speak fluently in terminology that parents are encountering for the first time. Accommodation requests may go unacknowledged. Parents may sense their child needs more support without knowing exactly how to ask for it, or what they’re even entitled to request.

This service is designed for those moments — helping families come into school conversations better informed, more prepared, and with a clearer sense of what is available for their child and how to ask for it.

Understanding the system is the first step to navigating it more effectively.

Why This Is Hard

The school system has its own language — and parents are expected to navigate it without a guide.

Ontario’s school system has formal processes for identifying, planning, and supporting students with learning differences or special education needs. These processes — IEPs, IPRCs, accommodation reviews, and school meetings — involve documentation, timelines, and decisions that have real consequences for a child’s educational experience.

Yet families are often expected to engage with these processes without preparation, without explanation, and without a clear sense of what they have the right to ask for. The result can be meetings that feel intimidating, IEPs that don’t reflect what a child actually needs, and accommodations that exist on paper but never make it into the classroom.

Being better informed going into these conversations changes the dynamic — not by making the process adversarial, but by making it more equitable.

“I had the assessment. I had the report. But I still didn’t know what to ask for or how to ask for it when I got into that meeting.”

Following through after the meeting

Ensuring accommodations are actually implemented and knowing what to do when they aren’t being used in the classroom.

How We Support Families

Services may include:

Support is tailored to each family’s situation. The goal is to help families feel better informed and more confident when navigating educational planning discussions.

reviewing existing IEP documents — helping parents understand what is in the document, what it means in practice, and whether it reflects their child’s current needs and profile.

preparing families for school meetings — reviewing the agenda, identifying key questions to ask, and helping parents understand what decisions may be made and how to participate effectively.

explaining school accommodation processes — clarifying how accommodations are requested, reviewed, and implemented within Ontario’s education system and what families can ask for.

supporting communication with schools — helping families draft follow-up correspondence, frame concerns professionally, and maintain a productive working relationship with the school.

helping families understand their child’s educational rights and supports — explaining what families can ask for, what the school is required to provide, and how the process works in Ontario.

attending school meetings virtually when appropriate — in some cases, a clinician may join a school meeting virtually to provide real-time support and help families navigate the discussion as it happens.

💡

The goal of this service

To help families feel better informed and more confident when navigating educational planning discussions — so they can participate as informed advocates for their child.

How many sessions will I need?

This depends entirely on your family’s situation. Some families need a single consultation to review an IEP and prepare for an upcoming meeting. Others benefit from several sessions over the course of a school year as they navigate ongoing accommodation discussions, transitions, or IPRC processes.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Is this legal representation? Can you advocate with the school board? ▼
No — this is a consultation and guidance service, not legal advocacy or legal representation. Our clinicians help you understand your child’s rights, prepare for meetings, and navigate school processes with greater confidence. If your situation requires formal legal representation in a dispute with a school board, we will say so clearly and will not represent this service as meeting that need.
Can a clinician attend our school meeting with us? ▼
In some cases, yes. Attending school meetings virtually when appropriate is part of this service. Whether this makes sense depends on the nature of the meeting, the family’s goals, and the specific situation. This is discussed during the consultation process, and virtual attendance is arranged where it is appropriate and helpful.
My child doesn’t have a formal diagnosis. Can we still use this service? ▼
Yes. A formal diagnosis is not required. Many families access this service because they are navigating a suspected learning challenge, the school has raised concerns, or an IEP is being developed. A formal assessment is helpful context but is not a prerequisite for consultation.
What is an IEP and how is it different from an IPRC? ▼
An IEP (Individual Education Plan) is a document that outlines the accommodations, modifications, and supports in place for a student. An IPRC (Identification, Placement, and Review Committee) is a formal committee meeting that identifies a student as exceptional and determines their educational placement. Both involve parents and have important implications — and helping families understand both processes is part of what this service provides.
How many sessions will I need? ▼
This depends entirely on your situation. Some families need one session to review an IEP before an upcoming meeting. Others benefit from several sessions over the course of a school year as they navigate ongoing accommodation discussions or transitions. The number of sessions is discussed at intake and reviewed as the situation evolves. There is no minimum commitment.
Will you contact the school on our behalf? ▼
Our clinicians can help you draft and review correspondence, prepare for communications, and think through how to approach conversations with the school. Direct contact with the school on a family’s behalf is not a standard part of this service, but the scope of what is helpful is discussed during the consultation process.
Is this covered by OHIP or insurance? ▼
Private pay services. Not covered by OHIP. Clients are responsible for confirming insurance coverage with their own insurer. Receipts are provided for reimbursement. All fees are confirmed in writing before any commitment is made.
What if this service isn’t the right fit for our situation? ▼
The guidance call is genuinely designed to answer that question honestly. If we believe another service — within Toriven Health or externally — would better support your family, we will say so clearly. We do not enrol families whose situations fall outside the scope of what this service can appropriately provide.
