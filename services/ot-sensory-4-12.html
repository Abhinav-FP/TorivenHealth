Occupational Therapy Programs for Children — Ontario

Sensory Processing · Fine Motor & Handwriting · Feeding Therapy · Ages 4–12

Occupational therapy helps children develop the practical skills and strategies needed to feel more comfortable, capable, and confident in their daily lives. Our programs support children across Ontario through virtual-first care, with individualized guidance for families.

Age Range Occupational therapy services are available for children approximately 4 years and older, with support tailored to developmental stage. Age ranges vary slightly by program and are confirmed during intake.

A formal diagnosis is not required to access occupational therapy. The guidance call is designed to help families understand whether a program may be appropriate for their child — before any commitment is made.

What Families Often Experience

When everyday activities feel harder than they should.

For most children, getting dressed, sitting in a classroom, holding a pencil, or trying a new food are unremarkable parts of the day. For some children, these same activities take enormous effort — and the struggle is often invisible to the people around them.

Occupational therapy meets children where they are. It focuses on the specific functional challenges affecting a child’s daily life — at home, at school, and in social situations — and helps develop practical strategies that make those activities more manageable over time.

No diagnosis is required. What matters is what the child is finding difficult — and whether OT can help.

Who Occupational Therapy May Help

Children may benefit if they experience…

A formal diagnosis is not required to access occupational therapy. What matters is what the child is finding difficult in their daily life.

Sensory sensitivities to noise, lights, textures, or movement — becoming overwhelmed in environments that others find manageable, or seeking constant sensory stimulation.

Difficulty with handwriting or fine motor coordination — struggling with pencil grip, letter formation, cutting, or any task requiring precise hand and finger control.

Challenges with attention or classroom participation — difficulty sitting still, staying focused, or regulating behaviour in structured classroom environments.

Emotional overwhelm in busy environments — meltdowns, shutdowns, or withdrawal in sensory-rich settings like playgrounds, lunchrooms, or shopping centres.

Limited food preferences or sensory-based feeding challenges — a very restricted range of accepted foods, strong reactions to textures or tastes, or significant mealtime stress.

Difficulty with daily routine independence — dressing, personal hygiene, or household tasks that are significantly harder for the child than would be expected for their age.

No diagnosis required

Many children who benefit from OT have a known diagnosis, while others are simply experiencing functional challenges that affect their daily participation. 🏠

Increased independence with daily routines

Getting dressed, managing belongings, completing morning and bedtime routines with less adult support — and more confidence.

✏️

Improved handwriting and school participation

Writing that is less effortful, more legible, and no longer a source of frustration — so children can focus on what they’re learning, not how their hand feels.

🧘

Fewer meltdowns, reduced avoidance of everyday environments, and calmer responses to sensory input that previously caused significant distress.

🧒

Children who feel more capable in their bodies are often more willing to engage in play, try new activities, and participate alongside peers without anxiety about keeping up.

🍽️

Routines that were previously a source of daily conflict or anxiety become more manageable — reducing family stress alongside the child’s own experience of their day.

How Virtual Occupational Therapy Works

Your child’s home is the best environment for OT.

Virtual occupational therapy allows our clinicians to observe your child within their natural environment — home. Many families are surprised at how much can be assessed and supported through a well-structured virtual session.

ℹ️

Virtual OT is not a compromise. Observing a child in their own home — with their actual furniture, lighting, routines, and daily triggers present — often provides clinicians with richer, more actionable information than a clinical office setting.

Sessions may include…

🎮

Reviewing play and movement activities

Clinicians observe how your child moves, reaches, grasps, and coordinates — through age-appropriate tasks guided by the parent in real time.

✏️

Observing fine motor skills

Parents guide their child through writing, drawing, or cutting tasks so the clinician can assess pencil grip, letter formation, pressure, and hand endurance.

📅

Reviewing daily routines

Discussing or observing morning routines, mealtimes, homework time, and transitions gives clinicians insight into where the real functional challenges occur.

🤝

Guiding parents through strategies

Parents participate actively — receiving coaching on specific techniques and home activities to continue between sessions for consistent skill-building.

🔍

Identifying environmental triggers

Clinicians may ask to see the child’s study space, seating, lighting, noise levels, and sensory inputs — recommending practical adjustments to reduce overwhelm.

Environmental triggers clinicians may review…

Noise levels and acoustic environment
Lighting conditions (natural and artificial)
Seating setup and desk height for writing tasks
Clutter and visual sensory load in the workspace
Meal environment and food presentation
Pencil type, grip tools, and writing surface

What families receive

Families typically receive a clinical summary and written recommendations that can be applied immediately at home and shared with school environments. This may include sensory processing observations, functional skill recommendations, home routine strategies, and classroom support suggestions.

Where appropriate, families may share recommendations with teachers, school support teams, family physicians, or other clinicians involved in the child’s care.

Virtual OT is available to families across all of Ontario

No travel required. Sessions happen from your home, on your schedule. This makes OT accessible to families in regions where in-person services are limited or waitlists are long.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Does my child need a diagnosis to access OT? ▼
No. A formal diagnosis is not required to access occupational therapy. Many children who benefit have a known diagnosis such as ADHD, autism, or developmental coordination disorder — but many others are simply experiencing functional challenges without a formal label. What matters is whether your child is struggling with everyday activities and whether OT can meaningfully help.
How does virtual OT actually work? ▼
Virtual OT sessions happen via secure video. Your clinician observes your child directly — watching how they move, hold objects, write, interact with food, or respond to sensory input. Parents guide their child through activities during the session, and the clinician coaches in real time. Many OT services are highly effective virtually, including sensory assessment, fine motor observation, feeding guidance, and home environment review.
Can my child be in more than one program? ▼
Possibly. Some children have overlapping challenges — for example, sensory sensitivities that also affect feeding, or fine motor difficulties alongside attention and regulation challenges. Whether a child is supported by one or more programs is determined during the guidance call and intake process, based on the child’s profile and what would be most useful and manageable for the family.
Is OT covered by OHIP or insurance? ▼
Occupational therapy is not covered by OHIP. However, OT is often covered under extended health benefits plans. Clients are responsible for confirming their own coverage with their insurer. Receipts are provided for reimbursement purposes. All fees are confirmed in writing before any commitment is made.
What age range does OT support? ▼
Our OT programs typically support children ages 4–12, with some variation by program. The Sensory Processing & Regulation and Feeding Therapy programs typically support ages 4–12 and 4–10 respectively, while Fine Motor & Handwriting Development typically begins at age 5. Age appropriateness is confirmed during the guidance call based on the child’s individual profile and developmental stage.
How many sessions will my child need? ▼
Session frequency and program length depend on the child’s specific challenges, goals, and progress. This is discussed during the intake process and reviewed regularly throughout the program. Families are not committed to a fixed number of sessions beyond what has been discussed and agreed upon in writing.
Will we receive written recommendations? ▼
Yes. Families typically receive a clinical summary and recommendations following assessment, which may include sensory processing observations, functional skill recommendations, home routine strategies, and classroom support suggestions. These can be shared with teachers, school support teams, the family physician, or other clinicians involved in the child’s care where appropriate.
What if OT isn’t the right fit for my child? ▼
The guidance call is genuinely designed to answer that question honestly. If we believe a different service would better support your child — whether within Toriven Health or externally — we will say so clearly. We do not enrol children whose situations fall outside the scope of what our programs can appropriately provide.

Program 1 — Sensory Processing & Regulation Support

Ages 4–12
Occupational Therapy · Sensory Program

Helping Children Feel Comfortable in Their Environment

Some children experience sensory input more intensely than others. Everyday environments such as classrooms, playgrounds, or busy homes can feel overwhelming — not as a behavioural choice, but because of how the nervous system receives and responds to sensory information.

Occupational therapy helps children develop strategies to manage sensory experiences and regulate their responses to the environment, building skills that support participation in school, home routines, and social activities.

What Is Sensory Processing?

Sensory processing is how the nervous system receives and responds to the environment.

Children who experience sensory processing challenges may respond to everyday sensory input — sound, touch, light, movement — more intensely or differently than expected. The challenge is neurological in nature, not a choice or a behaviour problem.

Sensory differences are common in children with ADHD, autism, anxiety, and developmental differences — but children do not need a formal diagnosis to benefit from support.

Children may…

Become overwhelmed in noisy environments
Avoid certain textures or clothing
Have strong reactions to lights or sounds
Seek constant movement or stimulation

Coping skills for overwhelming environments

Program 2 — Fine Motor & Handwriting Development

Ages 5–12
Occupational Therapy · Fine Motor Program

Helping Children Build Confidence With Writing

Handwriting and fine motor coordination are essential for classroom participation. Some children struggle with writing not because of effort or ability, but because the underlying motor skills — grip, endurance, letter formation, hand-eye coordination — have not developed in the way expected for their age.

Occupational therapy helps children develop the motor coordination and endurance needed for classroom writing tasks — and the confidence that comes from being able to complete school work without it being a physical struggle.

Why Some Children Struggle With Writing

Handwriting difficulty is often a motor challenge, not a learning one.

Handwriting requires the integration of multiple physical skills: hand strength, fine motor dexterity, pencil grip, postural stability, visual-motor coordination, and sustained hand endurance. When any of these is underdeveloped, writing becomes difficult and tiring — even for a capable, intelligent child.

Common signs include weak pencil grip, hand fatigue after short periods of writing, poor letter formation, slow writing speed, and avoidance of written tasks. These can affect classroom participation, assignment completion, and a child’s confidence at school.

Some children struggle with…

Weak pencil grip
Hand fatigue during writing
Poor letter formation or inconsistent sizing
Slow writing speed relative to peers

Skills Developed

Building the motor foundation for school success

✊

Pencil grip and hand strength

Developing a functional grip and the hand strength needed to sustain writing tasks without fatigue or pain.

🔤

Letter formation and printing

Building consistent, legible letter formation and spatial awareness on a page — at a pace appropriate to the child’s development.

🎯

Hand-eye coordination

Strengthening the visual-motor integration needed to place letters accurately and copy from the board.

⏱️

Writing speed and endurance

Gradually building the physical endurance to sustain writing tasks for the duration expected in a classroom setting.

Program 3 — Feeding Therapy & Food Exploration

Ages 4–10
Occupational Therapy · Feeding Program

Supporting Children With Feeding Challenges

For some families, mealtime can become stressful when children avoid certain foods or have strong reactions to textures and tastes. This is rarely a behavioural issue. Feeding challenges often have a sensory basis — children who find certain textures, smells, or presentations overwhelming may genuinely be unable to tolerate foods that others find unremarkable.

Feeding therapy helps children gradually build comfort with new foods and develop healthier eating patterns — through a collaborative, warm, and pressure-free approach that honours the child’s pace.

Who May Benefit

Feeding challenges are more common than families often realize.

Selective eating and food avoidance in children is often misunderstood as fussiness or a parenting issue. In many cases, it has a clear sensory basis — the texture, smell, visual appearance, or temperature of food genuinely triggers an overwhelming physical response in the child.

Without support, food repertoires can narrow over time as children avoid more and more foods to prevent that discomfort. Feeding therapy offers a gradual, supportive pathway toward greater flexibility.

Children may benefit if they…

Eat a very limited range of foods
Avoid foods due to texture sensitivity
Experience anxiety around new or unfamiliar foods
Struggle with transitions during meals

Our Approach

Gradual, collaborative, pressure-free.

Feeding therapy begins with where the child is, not where they “should” be. The goal is to build positive associations with food exploration and reduce the anxiety, avoidance, or distress that makes mealtimes difficult.

Progress is incremental — expanding comfort one small step at a time — and the pace is always guided by the child. Outcomes vary and no specific dietary changes are promised or guaranteed.

🤝

Collaborative Approach

Feeding therapy may involve collaboration between Occupational Therapists and Speech-Language Pathologists, allowing clinicians to support both sensory tolerance and oral-motor skills where both are relevant.
